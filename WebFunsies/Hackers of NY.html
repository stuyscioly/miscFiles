Hackers of NY

The New Yorkers are hacking. Rep the Silicon Alley.

“The definition of ‘hack’ has recently morphed and I’m really happy about that. The the term is no longer like back in my dad’s day – four dudes in a dark basement, like in that cheesy movie Hackers. David Lee of SV Angel actually wanted us to change [Tech@NYU’s] name to Hack@NYU - he was like, you need to change what that word means to society. To hack means to be resourceful, to take a look at a problem and be creative in your approach. I’ve never been a hacker in the traditional sense. I’m the world’s most inefficient developer, but what I think I’ve been doing these past couple of years is hacking ‘community’ and hacking how people relate to one another. A lot of my side projects have been about scaling human experience and sharing.”

Kim Pham just graduated from NYU with a degree in Marketing, Information Systems, and Computer Science. During her time there, she was president of NYC’s largest student designer & developer organization, Tech@NYU, and founding partner at Dorm Room Fund. She is now taking her love of the Internet and supporting talented creators overseas to Europe, where she is joining the Frontline Ventures team as Head of Platform.

"I love cover songs and I wanted to see how they would compare to each other from a relationship standpoint and also from a simple visual perspective. So I took a look at how the graph connections cluster in associating related songs based on a theme, as a node-edge map, and I also made a bunch of spectrograms to see frequency distribution for source and cover songs. I wanted to see how similar they looked and how closely they ghosted each other because a lot of people are like "oh, creativity isn’t copied and no one really likes cover bands because they’re really lame and they can’t come up with their own ideas" but we’re never really coming up with our own ideas. We’re always copy coding other people’s work. At some point, you’ve copied a design pattern or implemented someone else’s algorithm even in your most original projects, that’s the kind of open source creative culture we’ve built around code. You’re always viewing source pages on the web to see how someone else is doing something; and that attraction to learning from a source to re:creatively interpret an idea is really fascinating to me as a FOSS super-fan."

Aurelia started a radio show called Stereo Semantics where she analyzes the connections between a source song and its cover by building social graphs between them, like six degrees of separation but for radio, or like the semantic web. She also runs the hub chapter of Girl Develop It and is a Knight-Mozilla Open News Fellow developing open source crisis mapping software. | http://algorhyth.ms/ | www.stereosemantics.com

“I think the most interesting thing about Bitcoin is the fact that it’s not just a currency, it’s a protocol that you can use to build applications. So moving away from the traditional client-server model where you have one server hosting a website and you depend on that server’s resources for RAM, storage, bandwidth, you can move away from that and go towards a decentralized network to host your application. And that involves using the user’s resources to power the application itself. So instead of relying on one server and its resources, you rely on client’s hard drive space, bandwidth, and RAM. So that would mean the more users you have, the more powerful your whole network is, so the faster your application goes, as opposed to the traditional model where the more users you have the more strain you have on the system.”

The Hack: An open source “pastebin for images”. When the source code is released anyone will be able to host it and have their own private image sharing site. | http://temp.pics

TL;DR These guys are hacking education, giving underprivileged NYC students extra help to test into specialized high schools.

"I was the one who taught her. She was just struggling so badly, she was so far behind that we had to pull her out of class and work with her one-on-one.

I just remember she had the hardest time doing long division, even when she was subtracting she had to use her fingers, and she’s what, like 12, 13 and she doesn’t know how to subtract?

Before she took Student to Student, she was getting under 100 out of 800 on the SHSAT but then when she took the test, she got over 300. That is the most amazing thing I’ve ever seen.”

NYC 8th graders have to apply for high schools by taking the SHSAT. Those who can’t afford tutors and prep sessions fall behind. These guys built and run a free prep program for schools around NYC. Hackers of education.

Andy, Steven and Anna | Student to Student
